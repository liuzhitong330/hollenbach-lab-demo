Why Is MHC Assembly Hard? A Look at the HLA Polymorphism Landscape

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
HLA immunogenomics MHC assembly MHConstructor IPD-IMGT/HLA bioinformatics Python

The Hollenbach lab builds bioinformatics tools for one of the hardest regions in the human genome: the Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC). Their recently published MHConstructor (Genome Biology, 2024) is, to date, the only self-contained pipeline for de novo MHC assembly from short-read sequencing data. The key insight is that standard assemblers fail on MHC because of a combination of extreme allelic polymorphism and large structural variation — MHConstructor addresses this with haplotype binning, separately assembling reads attributable to each of the two diploid haplotypes before scaffolding to a haplotype reference.

I was curious what that polymorphism landscape actually looks like in numbers, so I pulled real allele counts from the IPD-IMGT/HLA database (release 3.64.0, 46,005 total alleles) and built two figures to visualize the scale of the problem.

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. All allele count data are fetched directly from the official IPD-IMGT/HLA GitHub release (ANHIG/IMGTHLA). Growth milestone data are from Robinson J et al., "25 years of the IPD-IMGT/HLA Database," HLA 2024; doi:10.1111/tan.15549.

How many alleles does each HLA locus have?

HLA-B alone has 11,110 known alleles — roughly one new allele discovered every few days. For a diploid sample, the two HLA-B alleles are drawn from this pool. When sequencing reads from both alleles land on the same genomic segment, a conventional assembler collapses them into a chimeric consensus, losing haplotype-specific variants. This is the core problem MHConstructor solves with haplotype binning: separate the reads by haplotype first, then assemble each independently.
Class II (DRB1, DPB1, DQB1) adds structural complexity on top of sequence polymorphism. The HLA class II region contains large insertions and deletions between haplotypes — for example, the DR52/DR53/DR51 supertypes involving DRB3/4/5 copy-number variation. MHConstructor generates intermediate haplotype-specific consensus sequences and scaffolds assemblies to the relevant haplotype reference to maintain structural integrity. This is why no generic assembler (SPAdes, Velvet, MEGAHIT) works well here without MHC-specific pre-processing.

The reference is always growing — static assemblies go stale

NGS after 2010 caused a 6-fold increase in known alleles by 2024 — and discovery is ongoing. This matters for assembly strategy: a pipeline that directly maps short reads to a fixed reference panel will misassemble reads from alleles discovered after the reference was built. MHConstructor's haplotype-informed scaffolding approach uses the reference to guide assembly order rather than as a mapping target, which is more robust to novel alleles. The ongoing growth also means that quality metrics (like the reported 0.031% base-call error rate in the Genome Biology 2024 paper) should be re-benchmarked as the reference expands.

An open question I find compelling

MHConstructor's haplotype binning accuracy depends on being able to distinguish reads from the two haplotypes. The harder case is a homozygous-like sample — where both HLA alleles at a given locus are very similar (e.g., differ at only 2–3 positions). In those samples, binning becomes ambiguous and mis-binned reads could corrupt the assembly of either haplotype.

A natural computational extension: can we predict, before assembly, which samples will be hardest for MHConstructor based on the pairwise sequence distance between their two haplotype alleles? This would require:

  1. Estimating haplotype identity from a rapid pre-pass (e.g., k-mer similarity between read sets)
  2. Flagging samples where the two haplotypes fall below a distance threshold
  3. Triggering a different assembly strategy — or longer read data — for those samples

The IMGT/HLA database already contains pairwise alignment data for each allele. Computing the distribution of minimum pairwise distances at each locus (especially HLA-B and DRB1, the most polymorphic) would give a direct estimate of how often this "near-identical haplotype" problem occurs in population cohorts.
